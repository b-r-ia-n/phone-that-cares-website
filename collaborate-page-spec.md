# Collaborate Page — Content Plan & Spec

## Page Structure (top to bottom)

### 1. Open Questions — Interactive Stickers

The top of the page. 5-6 clickable "hello my name is ___" style sticker/post-it elements. Each one is a prompt that, when clicked, opens a simple text field and a "Send" button (initially these can just generate a mailto: link to Brian's email with the prompt pre-filled as the subject line — no backend needed).

Visual style: hand-drawn-feeling post-it notes or stickers. Slightly rotated, casual placement. Each one has a different subtle color within the Sage & Stone palette.

**The 6 stickers:**

1. **"You should read ___"**
   Subtext: Book, paper, article, blog post — anything that shaped your thinking on attention or technology.

2. **"You'd find ___ interesting"**
   Subtext: A project, product, person, or community working on related ideas.

3. **"You should talk to me — I'm ___"**
   Subtext: Introduce yourself. Tell me what you work on and where to find you.

4. **"The thing you're missing is ___"**
   Subtext: Constructive criticism welcome. What's the blind spot?

5. **"I have a skill that could help: ___"**
   Subtext: Developer, designer, researcher, writer, filmmaker, organizer — all useful.

6. **"I'd use this if it ___"**
   Subtext: What would make this real for you? What feature or framing would make you pay attention?

### 3. Intellectual Lineage

Books, papers, and thinkers that shaped the project's thinking. Each entry has the title/author and 1-2 sentences of Brian's annotation explaining why it matters to this project specifically.

**Books:**
- *The Wright Brothers* — David McCullough
  → The framing of studying what healthy flight/attention looks like (how things that fly actually work) rather than just pathologizing crashes. The brothers studied birds, not wreckage.

- *Addiction by Design: Machine Gambling in Las Vegas* — Natasha Dow Schüll
  → The most detailed account of how physical environments are engineered to sustain engagement. Slot machines as the prototype for digital attention capture. The "machine zone" concept.

- *E Unibus Pluram: Television and U.S. Fiction* — David Foster Wallace
  → The essay that identified television's recursive trap: it absorbs and neutralizes the very critiques aimed at it. Social media does the same thing, faster.

- *The Back of the Napkin* — Dan Roam
  → Visual thinking as a way to make complex systems legible. Direct inspiration for the diagram-heavy approach of this project.

**Research & papers:**
- The Rat Park experiments — Bruce K. Alexander
  → The finding that addiction is primarily about environment, not substance. Rats in enriched environments don't become addicted. The phone is simultaneously the drug and the potential Rat Park.

- QRI's logarithmic scales of wellbeing
  → Structural model for the Five Levels of Phone Caring essay. The idea that qualitative differences in kind can be mapped onto a continuous scale.

- Processed food literature (Monteiro, Hall, et al.)
  → The "degree of processing" frame that Brian applies to digital content. Ultra-processed food as analog for ultra-processed media. The most predictive variable isn't content type, it's processing degree.

**Design & technology thinking:**
- Forethought.org — "Design Sketches: Angels on the Shoulder"
  → The closest existing vision document to what this project is building. AI-powered tools that help people make more endorsed decisions in real time. Aligned recommender systems, guardian angels, reflection scaffolding.

- Calm Technology — Amber Case
  → Technology that informs without demanding attention. The periphery as a design space.

- Visakan Veerasamy's marketing/framing work
  → Influence on how Brian thinks about communicating the project's vision and building community around it.

**Cultural / philosophical:**
- The concept of *ma* (間) in Japanese aesthetics
  → Negative space, the pause, the interval. A design principle for what a phone could offer between moments of content consumption.

- Amish technology adoption practices
  → A community that evaluates technology by asking "what will this do to our community?" rather than "what can this do?" — a deliberative model for technology adoption.

### 4. Related Projects & Kindred Spirits

Other people and organizations building in adjacent or overlapping spaces. This section positions Brian's project within a broader movement and helps visitors discover related work.

**Hardware / alternative phones:**
- **Light Phone** (thelightphone.com) — The most prominent "less phone" product. Beautiful industrial design, radical minimalism. Brian's project diverges here: Light Phone removes functionality, while Phone That Cares maintains full capability and adds a supportive layer. An important reference point for what restriction-based approaches look like at their best.

- **Mudita** (mudita.com) — Polish company making minimalist phones and wellness-focused devices. Similar philosophy to Light Phone but with a more explicit wellness framing.

- **Minimal Phone** — E-ink device with a physical keyboard. BlackBerry-inspired form factor for the distraction-free market.

**Software / apps:**
- **Opal** — Screen time management app. Represents the "restriction-based" approach Brian's project critiques but also learns from.

- **one sec** — App that introduces a brief pause before opening social media. Closer to Brian's "integration over restriction" philosophy than most tools in this space.

- **Brick** — Physical NFC token that blocks apps when tapped. Interesting because it adds a physical ritual to the digital intervention.

**Research & advocacy organizations:**
- **Center for Humane Technology** (Tristan Harris, Aza Raskin) — The most visible organization in the "attention economy" critique space. Their framing tends toward alarm/advocacy; Brian's project is more about building alternatives.

- **Forethought.org** — Research organization sketching AI-enabled tools for better decision-making. The "Angels on the Shoulder" design sketches are directly relevant.

- **All Tech Is Human** — Nonprofit building the responsible tech pipeline. Community and job board connecting people working on tech ethics and wellbeing.

**Writers / thinkers in this space:**
- **Jenny Odell** — *How to Do Nothing: Resisting the Attention Economy*. Artistic/philosophical approach to attention and resistance.

- **Cal Newport** — *Digital Minimalism*, *Deep Work*. Productivity-focused framing of attention management. More restriction-oriented than Brian's approach but widely influential.

- **Nir Eyal** — *Hooked* then *Indistractable*. Interesting because he wrote the playbook for building habit-forming products, then wrote the counter-playbook. Represents the "willpower" framing Brian critiques.

- **L.M. Sacasas** — *The Convivial Society* newsletter. Deeper philosophical engagement with technology and human flourishing. Ivan Illich-influenced.

**Presentation:** Each entry has a name, a one-line description, a link, and optionally a sentence about how it relates to this project (where it aligns, where it diverges). Could be organized as a simple list grouped by category, or as a more visual card-based layout.

## Font

All text on this page (and the entire site) uses **Inter** — no serif fonts anywhere. Load from Google Fonts, weights 400, 500, and 600. Body text is 400, nav labels are 400 (inactive) / 500 (active), headings are 600. No other font families should appear on the site.

## Design Notes

- This page uses the reserved **Slate** color theme (#DCDFE0 background, #3B4A52 accent) — it's the most "functional/action-oriented" of the reserved colors, which fits a page that's asking people to do something.
- The stickers at the top should feel inviting and slightly playful — they're the most interactive element on the entire site.
- The "People I've Been Thinking With" section should feel warm and generous — it's a thank-you note, not a credentials list.
- The intellectual lineage and related projects sections should feel like a reading list you'd share with a friend, not an academic bibliography.

## Adding This Tab

In `tabs.ts`, add:

```ts
{
  slug: 'collaborate',
  label: 'Collaborate',
  description: 'Open questions, community, and intellectual lineage.',
  theme: 'collaborate',
  order: 5,
},
```

In `themes.css`, add:

```css
[data-theme="collaborate"] {
  --bg: #DCDFE0;
  --accent: #3B4A52;
  --accent-muted: rgba(59, 74, 82, 0.45);
  --border-pill: rgba(59, 74, 52, 0.15);
  --text-body: #2B3B45;
  --text-muted: rgba(59, 74, 82, 0.6);
}
```
